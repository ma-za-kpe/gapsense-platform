# Initial Market and User Research Brief

**Date:** 2026-07-22
**Status:** Primary-source desk research; hypotheses require direct user validation

## Research Question

What should a web-first GapSense validate in Ghana and Uganda before channel expansion or
deployment?

## What the Evidence Says

### The problem is not merely access to curriculum content

Ghana's Differentiated Learning Plus programme combines targeted instruction, structured
pedagogy, play-based learning, and real-time progression tracking. UNICEF reports evidence
from a three-year randomized design across 320 primary schools. This supports GapSense's
root-gap and targeted-intervention direction, but it also means the product must demonstrate
incremental value beyond another curriculum content library or dashboard.

Source: [UNICEF - Improving foundational learning at scale in Ghana](https://www.unicef.org/documents/improving-foundational-learning-scale-evidence-ghanas-differentiated-learning-plus)

Uganda's 2024 Learning Poverty Brief reports that 82% of children at late-primary age were
not proficient in reading, adjusted for out-of-school children, using the latest available
pre-COVID learning data. The age of the underlying data must be stated whenever this figure
is used.

Source: [World Bank - Uganda Learning Poverty Brief 2024](https://documents.worldbank.org/en/publication/documents-reports/documentdetail/099062624143010228)

### Digital readiness is uneven; excellent web UX must include constrained conditions

UNICEF's 2025 Ghana implementation research frames successful digital learning around
appropriateness, adoption, fidelity, and feasibility in real low-resource classrooms. That
is a useful evaluation frame for GapSense: technical correctness alone is insufficient.

Source: [UNICEF Innocenti - Digital Learning, Real Classrooms](https://www.unicef.org/innocenti/reports/digital-learning-real-classrooms)

Uganda's 2026 nationwide secondary-school digital-readiness reporting says more than a
third of surveyed schools had no internet, only about 12% had campus-wide access, and the
average was one digital device per 63 students. These figures are recent but apply to the
surveyed secondary-school context, not every target user.

Source: [UNICEF Uganda - Uganda's digital education gap](https://www.unicef.org/uganda/stories/ugandas-digital-education-gap)

The Government of Uganda and UNICEF have also used Kolibri for online and offline learning,
including accessibility-oriented formats. This makes offline-capable public infrastructure
a potential integration or learning source, not merely a competitor.

Source: [UNICEF Uganda - Kolibri rollout](https://www.unicef.org/uganda/stories/unicef-and-government-uganda-roll-out-kolibri-platform)

### The addressable education systems are large and institutionally complex

Uganda's official 2025 Education Statistics Abstract reports more than 9 million primary
learners in public provision and thousands of secondary schools reporting through EMIS.
This shows scale, but it does not establish an immediately serviceable market or willingness
to adopt GapSense.

Source: [Uganda Ministry of Education and Sports - Education Statistics Abstract 2025](https://www.education.go.ug/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/UGANDAS-EDUCATION-STATISTICAL-ABSTRACT_2025.pdf)

Ghana is actively investing in foundational learning monitoring, including the 2025
Foundational Learning Action Tracker. GapSense should research interoperability and
partnership opportunities before positioning itself as a replacement for national efforts.

Source: [UNICEF Ghana - Foundational Learning Action Tracker](https://www.unicef.org/ghana/press-releases/ghana-accelerates-foundational-learning-ministry-education-unicef-and-hempel)

## Initial Product Hypotheses

These are hypotheses, not decisions:

1. **Teacher-first is likely stronger than learner-direct initially.** Teachers can supply
   context, interpret uncertainty, and protect against unsafe automated conclusions.
2. **Curriculum intelligence may be the entry wedge.** A trustworthy prerequisite explorer
   and diagnostic planning tool can demonstrate value before image or conversational AI.
3. **Low-bandwidth and resumability are core features.** They should be measured in browser
   tests, not left for later optimization.
4. **Offline capability may matter more than sophisticated generation.** User research must
   test this by role and setting.
5. **The product should complement existing national programmes.** Integration, evidence,
   and teacher workflow support may be more credible than a standalone replacement claim.
6. **Country-specific structure is a product advantage.** Ghana's five key phases and
   Uganda's thematic-to-subject transition create different diagnostic and UX requirements.
7. **The likely user and buyer are often different.** Teachers may create daily value while
   schools, programmes, NGOs, districts, or ministries control budgets.
8. **Curriculum intelligence may be independently monetizable.** Other education products may
   value a validated curriculum/prerequisite API, but licensing and demand remain untested.

## Research Gaps Before MVP Scope Is Frozen

- Which users feel the root-gap problem most acutely and currently attempt to solve it?
- What artifacts do teachers already use to diagnose learning gaps?
- How much time can a teacher spend creating or reviewing a diagnostic?
- Which decisions require source transparency and which require a simpler summary?
- What device is available to the actual operator, and is it shared?
- When and where is connectivity available?
- Which language is used to teach, assess, discuss misconceptions, and communicate results?
- How do existing assessment, EMIS, curriculum, and digital-learning tools fit the workflow?
- What would make teachers distrust or abandon an AI-assisted diagnosis?
- Who can authorize pilots and use of learner data?
- What adoption model is viable across public, private, NGO, and household settings?
- Who pays, if anyone, and only after what value has been demonstrated?
- What measurable savings or outcomes justify recurring rather than one-time payment?
- What are procurement duration, implementation burden, support expectations, and renewal criteria?
- Which segment can sustain healthy gross margins without excluding the learners most in need?

## Required Field Research

Before freezing the minimum lovable product:

- interview at least five educators in each country;
- include Primary and Secondary, public and private, and urban and rural contexts;
- include at least one curriculum specialist per country;
- observe at least two real diagnostic or remediation planning workflows per country;
- test a low-fidelity curriculum explorer and diagnostic flow;
- record devices, connectivity, language, time, trust, privacy, and accessibility constraints;
- add findings and follow-up tasks to the canonical working list.

## Competitive and Partner Landscape Work Still Required

The next research slice should map:

- national curriculum and teacher-support portals;
- differentiated-learning and formative-assessment programmes;
- school information and assessment systems;
- digital content and offline learning platforms;
- AI tutoring and teacher-assistant products serving either country;
- NGOs, universities, teacher organizations, and government programmes that could validate
  or distribute GapSense.

Each entry must distinguish competitor, complement, infrastructure, and potential partner.
No TAM, revenue, or pricing claim should be promoted until adoption and buyer research is
completed.
